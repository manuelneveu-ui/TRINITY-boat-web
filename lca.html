Life cycle analysis

Environmental footprint of the Trinity hull

Composite boats are lightweight and efficient, but their materials and manufacturing steps are energy‑intensive and emit greenhouse gases over the boat’s life cycle.

The LCA of Trinity focuses on the composite hull of our 70 cm RC speedboat and helps us identify the main environmental hotspots and eco‑design options for future versions.

Studied system

Functional unit

One 70 cm‑long remote‑controlled speedboat hull, made of carbon fiber and polyester resin, designed for repeated 20‑minute runs.

Main materials

  • Carbon fiber and polyester resin composite for the hull shell.
  • PLA used for the 3D‑printed mould.

Manufacturing steps

  • 3D printing of the PLA mould.
  • Composite lay‑up and vacuum bagging.
  • Finishing and cleaning of the hull.

Goal and scope

  • Quantify the environmental impact of the composite hull.
  • Identify environmental hotspots in the life cycle.
  • Explore eco‑design improvement options for Trinity.

The study considers one hull manufactured and used in France over 2025–2026.

System boundaries

Included processes

  • Production of composite raw materials (carbon fiber, resin).
  • PLA production for the mould.
  • Electricity used during manufacturing.
  • Use of acetone and water for processing and cleaning.
  • End‑of‑life treatment of composite and plastic waste.

Excluded processes

  • Motor, battery, electronics, propeller and shaft.
  • Transport and maintenance during use.

The LCA therefore isolates the impact of the hull itself, without the rest of the RC system.

Life cycle inventory

Key inputs

  • Carbon fiber reinforced plastic: 2.4 kg.
  • PLA granulate: 1 kg.
  • Acetone: 0.3 kg.
  • Electricity: 10 kWh for mould printing and processing.

These flows represent the materials and energy needed to manufacture a single Trinity hull.

Key outputs

  • VOC emissions to air.
  • Water consumption and wastewater.
  • Mixed plastic waste and polyester fibre waste at end‑of‑life.

Carbon fiber composite waste is approximated using mixed plastic waste treatment due to limited datasets for carbon fiber waste.

Life cycle inventory – main inputs (materials, energy) and outputs (emissions, waste) for one Trinity hull.

LCA modelling

The study was modelled in SimaPro using the Ecoinvent 3 cut‑off database and the ReCiPe impact assessment method, with life cycle networks and impact graphs generated for the hull system.

Across impact categories such as climate change, marine eutrophication, fossil resource use, water consumption and toxicity, the carbon fiber composite clearly dominates the footprint of the hull.

Improvement options and limitations

Eco‑design directions

  • Reduce composite material consumption and offcuts.
  • Optimise and reuse moulds to limit PLA and waste.
  • Reduce excess resin and improve manufacturing efficiency.
  • Use bio‑based or lower‑impact resins where feasible.
  • Use renewable electricity for energy‑intensive steps.

Study limitations

  • Only the hull was analysed, not the full boat system.
  • Some datasets are approximations from Ecoinvent.

These limits should be kept in mind when interpreting the results, but they still provide a solid basis for guiding more sustainable design choices on Trinity.
